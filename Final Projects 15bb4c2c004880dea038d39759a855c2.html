We use images from the Stanford Light Field Archive, which contains rectified images with spacing on a regularly spaced grid. Our goal is to use these images to mimic different focus and apertures.

Depth Adjustment

The far away parts of images don’t vary their position as drastically as the nearby parts of images. As such, if we average the image, we get “focus” on the back part of the image while a “blur” on the front. By shifting the alignment of the images, we are able to get focus in different places. We use an affine transformation to shift the images by their respective (u,v)(u,v)(u,v)﻿ defined by their position on the grid, and then multiply the shift by a scaling factor ccc﻿ to control location of blur.

We can also make a gif of these changes: [see google drive link at the top of the project]

Aperuture Adjustment

The aputure controls the radius of the focus. Therefore, by averaging a different number of images, we are able to control how focused/blurry the images are, therefore simulating a change in the radius of aputure. Here, we define the radius as the number of images we use, where we take a r∗rr*rr∗r﻿ images, centered at the ‘origin’ image.

Summary

We learned a lot about how depth of field interacts with images — namely, it was interesting to see how via depth of field, the closer an object is, the more it changes. Seeing the change mechanism visually via the blending procedure made the fact very obvious.

We also learned a lot about aperture. It was intuitive from the project that appature is proportional to the image blur — and thus the amount of averaging is proportional to the aperture.

Project 2: High-Dynamic Range

The problem statement for High-Dynamic Range (HDR) is the necessity of combining information across different images with different exposures to capture full dynamic range in the real world. Some modern cameras are unable to capture this level of detail, where the image will instead have too extreme of an exposure.

To accomplish this, we need to generate radiance map from a set of photographs, where each image is taken from the same center of projection but with varying exposures. Then, we take this radiance map to produce our final HDR images. Let’s take a look at a set of images as an example throughout this process.

Here we have images of an arch at different exposures. Let’s start by diving into the radiance map reconstruction.
